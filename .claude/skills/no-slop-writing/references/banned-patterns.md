# Banned Patterns

The complete slop blacklist. These are the documented tells of AI-generated text, the patterns Wikipedia editors, teachers, and editors have catalogued from thousands of samples. Every item here should be treated as radioactive: a single instance is forgivable in human writing, but they cluster, and clustering is what gets noticed.

For each pattern: what it looks like, why it happens, and what to do instead. The fix is never just deletion. Slop marks a spot where the writing went vague, so the fix is always *more specific content*.

## 1. Banned words

These words appear in AI text at many times their natural rate. Humans use some of them occasionally; AI reaches for them constantly.

**Verbs:** delve, boast (as in "the city boasts"), leverage, foster, harness, unleash, elevate, embark, navigate (metaphorically), spearhead, underscore, showcase, streamline, empower, unlock, revolutionize, transform (as hype), utilize (just say "use"), garner, encompass, facilitate.

**Adjectives:** vibrant, bustling, crucial, pivotal, robust, seamless, holistic, comprehensive, meticulous, intricate, nuanced, multifaceted, cutting-edge, ever-evolving, dynamic, innovative (unclaimed), compelling (unclaimed), invaluable, unparalleled, breathtaking, stunning (for anything that isn't literally stunning), notable, key (as in "key insights").

**Nouns:** tapestry, landscape (metaphorical), realm, journey (for anything that isn't travel), testament, beacon, cornerstone, game-changer, myriad, plethora, synergy, paradigm, insights (plural, vague), solutions (vague), stakeholders (outside actual business writing), individuals (just say "people").

**The fix:** each of these is a placeholder for a detail you didn't provide. "The vibrant food scene" becomes two named restaurants and what they serve. "Crucial for success" becomes what specifically breaks without it.

## 2. Banned phrases

Instant tells. Zero exceptions.

**Openers:**
- "In today's fast-paced / digital / ever-changing world..."
- "In the world of X..." / "In the realm of X..."
- "Have you ever wondered..." (rhetorical question openers generally)
- "Imagine a world where..."
- "When it comes to..."
- "Whether you're a beginner or an expert..." / any "whether you're X or Y"
- "From X to Y" as a fake-range flourish ("From bustling cities to serene landscapes")

**Mid-text filler:**
- "It's important to note that..." / "It's worth mentioning that..." (just note the thing)
- "plays a crucial/vital/key role in"
- "stands as a testament to"
- "is a testament to"
- "serves as" (usually just "is")
- "is home to" / "boasts"
- "rich cultural heritage" / "rich tapestry"
- "a wide range of" / "a variety of" (name three of them instead)
- "in order to" (just "to")
- "due to the fact that" (just "because")
- "at the end of the day"
- "needless to say" (then don't say it)
- "that being said" / "with that said"
- "look no further"
- "let's dive in" / "let's explore" / "deep dive"

**Closers:**
- "In conclusion," / "Overall," / "In summary," / "Ultimately," / "All in all,"
- "...and that makes all the difference."
- "The future looks bright for..."
- "Only time will tell."
- Any final paragraph that restates the piece. End on new material or end earlier.

## 3. Banned sentence constructions

These are grammatical templates, so they survive vocabulary swaps. They're the deepest tells.

### Negative parallelism: "It's not X, it's Y"

"It's not just a race, it's a lifestyle." "This isn't about winning; it's about growth." Also the reverse, and the "not only X but also Y" cousin. AI uses this to manufacture profundity. **Fix:** state Y directly and prove it. If the contrast genuinely matters, show it with facts, not a template.

### The participial tail

A main clause, comma, then an "-ing" phrase that bolts on unearned significance: "The team redesigned the hull, showcasing their commitment to innovation." "Sales rose 12%, highlighting the strategy's success." Research on instruction-tuned models found they use this construction at 2 to 5 times human rates. **Fix:** if the second half matters, give it its own sentence with its own evidence. Usually it doesn't matter. Cut it.

### The rule of three

"Creative, driven, and passionate." "Faster processing, cleaner data, and happier users." Triads everywhere, in adjectives, in phrases, in sentence sequences. One triad per piece is rhythm; a triad per paragraph is a metronome. **Fix:** break them. Use one strong item, or two, or four. Pick the single best one and develop it.

### Vague attribution (weasel wording)

"Experts say..." "Some critics argue..." "It has been described as..." "Many believe..." Opinions assigned to nobody. **Fix:** name the source or claim the opinion yourself. "Dr. Jeznach's 2023 survey found..." or "I think..." (The honest version of this move is the They Say / I Say structure in delivery-methods.md.)

### The equivocation seesaw

"While X presents challenges, it also offers opportunities." "However, others contend..." attached to every claim. Balanced-sounding sentences that cancel themselves out. **Fix:** decide what's true and say it. Acknowledge a real counterargument once, specifically, then answer it.

### The setup sentence

Sentences that promise content instead of delivering it: "This raises an important question." "There are several factors to consider." "Let's break this down." **Fix:** delete the promise, deliver the content.

### Symmetric "elegant variation"

Calling the same thing by rotating synonyms to avoid repetition: the boat, the vessel, the craft, the platform. Humans just repeat the word. **Fix:** repeat the word.

## 4. Banned structural habits

- **Uniform paragraphs.** Every paragraph 3 to 4 sentences, each ending with a mini-conclusion. Vary hard: let one paragraph run long and put a one-liner after it.
- **Bullet points in prose.** Essays, articles, blog posts, and emails are sentences. Bullets belong in actual reference docs and nowhere else unless requested.
- **Bold-term-colon lists.** "**Efficiency:** the system reduces..." is the most recognizable AI formatting pattern in existence.
- **Header mania.** Title Case Headers every 150 words, sections named "Challenges" and "Future Prospects" and "Conclusion." Short pieces need zero headers.
- **Mirroring the prompt.** Asked about "benefits and drawbacks," producing a Benefits section and a Drawbacks section. The structure should come from the ideas (pick one from delivery-methods.md), not from the assignment phrasing.
- **Front-loaded thesis + restated thesis.** Intro says what you'll say, body says it, conclusion says what you said. Say it once, well.
- **Emoji in headers or lists.** Never, unless the user's own style uses them.

## 5. Banned tones

- **Puffery.** Everything is "fascinating," "remarkable," "a must-see," "nestled in the heart of." Praise inflation makes all praise worthless. Reserve intensity for the one thing that earns it.
- **Uniform politeness.** Prose with no friction, no opinion, no risk. If a reader couldn't disagree with any sentence, the piece says nothing.
- **Scripted empathy.** "I understand this can be challenging." Emotion described instead of shown. Show the shaky hands, the 2 a.m. Slack ping, the specific thing. Or don't reach for emotion at all.
- **False authority hedging.** "may potentially help to possibly improve" is a triple-hedged claim. Hedge once, plainly, only when genuinely uncertain.
- **LinkedIn voice.** "Thrilled to announce." "Humbled and honored." "Key takeaways." Performed enthusiasm is slop in a blazer.

## 6. Punctuation and formatting tells

### Em dashes: banned

The em dash has become the single most recognized AI tell. Readers, teachers, and editors now treat it as a flag on sight, fairly or not, and AI overuses it badly enough that the suspicion is usually right. Default budget: **zero**.

Every em dash has a cleaner replacement, and choosing it usually improves the sentence:

- Dramatic pause or emphasis: use a period. "The robot held up. Six matches, zero failures." Short sentences carry emphasis better than punctuation does.
- Appended explanation: use a colon. "Clogs turned out to be the whole ballgame: every design change after June traces back to them."
- Mid-sentence aside: use parentheses (sparingly) or commas, or promote the aside to its own sentence.
- Interrupted contrast: restructure. "The design was cheap, and it worked" instead of a dash-hinged pivot.

The only exception is when editing a user's existing text where *they* wrote the dashes; match their style rather than purging their voice. Never introduce new ones.

### Everything else

- **Semicolon showing-off.** A semicolon every paragraph in casual writing reads as performance. Periods work.
- **Curly quotes / typographic apostrophes** in contexts where the user types straight ones. Match the user's typing.
- **Perfect punctuation in casual registers.** A text message or Discord post with flawless Oxford commas and zero lowercase is suspicious. Match the medium.

## The cluster test

None of these patterns alone proves anything; humans wrote them first, which is how they got into training data. What reads as AI is *density*. A finished piece should survive this audit: pick any 150-word stretch. If it contains more than one item from this file, it's not done.
