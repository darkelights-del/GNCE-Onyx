# Craft

Killing slop only gets writing to neutral. This file is what makes it good. The principles come from the writers people actually still read on writing (Zinsser, Orwell, Strunk and White, Stephen King, Paul Graham), compressed to what changes sentences in practice. For structure-level decisions, see delivery-methods.md; this file works at the sentence and paragraph level.

## 1. Clutter is the enemy (Zinsser)

Most first-draft sentences carry 20 to 40 percent dead weight: qualifiers, redundant pairs, prepositional chains, words that repeat what other words already said.

Before: "Personal experience has shown me that it is often quite difficult to be able to accurately predict exactly how a given experiment will actually turn out in practice."

After: "I can rarely predict how an experiment will turn out."

The mechanical version: hunt "very," "really," "quite," "rather," "actually," "basically," "in fact," "arguably," "somewhat," "a bit," "kind of." Cut 90% of them. Then hunt phrases doing one word's job: "in order to" (to), "has the ability to" (can), "at this point in time" (now), "despite the fact that" (although).

## 2. Prefer the short word, unless the long word is the right one (Orwell)

"Utilize" is use. "Commence" is start. "Endeavor" is try. "Individuals" is people. "Sufficient" is enough. Latinate vocabulary in casual contexts is one of the strongest AI signatures, and it also just reads worse.

The exception matters: when the precise word is long, use it. "Photometric stereo" shouldn't become "a light trick." Precision is the goal; short words are usually how you get there.

## 3. Concrete beats abstract, always

Abstract claims ask the reader to trust you. Concrete details let the reader see it themselves.

Before: "The competition was an incredible learning experience that taught me valuable lessons about perseverance and teamwork."

After: "We rebuilt the intake twice in the pit between matches. The second rebuild happened in eleven minutes because by then everyone knew exactly which screws came out first."

Rules of thumb: numbers beat adjectives ("$90 a month" beats "affordable"). Names beat categories ("Black Earth Compost" beats "a local composting partner"). One developed example beats three mentioned ones. If a paragraph has no proper noun, number, or physical detail, it's probably abstract mush. When the *topic itself* is abstract, use the ladder of abstraction in delivery-methods.md, section 11.

## 4. Verbs do the work

Weak sentences park the action in a noun and prop it up with "is," "has," or "makes."

- "made the decision to" (decided)
- "is indicative of" (shows)
- "conducted an analysis of" (analyzed)
- "provides a demonstration of" (demonstrates, or better, shows)

Prefer active voice by default: "the filter clogged," not "the filter was found to be clogged." But don't fetishize it. Passive is right when the actor is unknown or irrelevant: "the samples were contaminated" is fine if you don't know who did it.

## 5. Write like you talk, then tighten (Paul Graham)

Spoken language is the baseline register for almost everything: blogs, emails, essays, scripts. If you wouldn't say a sentence out loud, don't write it. Contractions are standard, not informal. "But" and "And" can start sentences. A fragment can land a point. Like this.

Tightening is the second half: spoken language rambles, so after drafting in a natural voice, cut the repetition and false starts that speech tolerates and print doesn't.

## 6. Rhythm: vary everything

Human writing has irregular sentence and paragraph lengths. Monotone rhythm is boring to read, and it's also the statistical signature detectors key on.

Bad rhythm (every sentence about 15 words, same shape):
"The robot performed well at the competition last weekend. The drivetrain held up through all six qualification matches. The new intake design scored consistently in autonomous mode. The team felt proud of the overall result."

Good rhythm:
"The robot held up. Six qualification matches, zero drivetrain failures, which after October felt like a miracle. The new intake was the real story: it scored in autonomous every single match, including the one where our alliance partner parked on top of our starting cone."

Notice the good version is also more *specific*. Rhythm and concreteness improve together because varied sentences give details room.

## 7. Have a point of view

Writing with no risk in it says nothing. Every good piece contains at least one sentence a reasonable reader could argue with: a claim, a preference, a call. Make it and back it.

Before: "There are various perspectives on whether school composting programs are effective, with proponents citing environmental benefits and critics noting logistical challenges."

After: "School composting programs mostly fail for one boring reason: nobody assigns a specific person to wheel the bins out on Thursday. Fix that and the rest is signage."

Hedging is allowed exactly where honesty requires it, stated once and plainly: "I don't know if this scales." Not: "It may potentially be the case that scaling could present certain challenges."

## 8. Openings and endings

**Open with the most interesting true thing.** A specific fact, a small scene, a claim someone could dispute. Never with context-setting ("Microplastics have become an increasing concern in recent years"), definitions, or a question you're about to answer yourself. Which *kind* of opening depends on the structure you picked in delivery-methods.md: a BLUF email opens with the ask, a feature opens with the scene, a news recap opens with the outcome.

Weak: "Plastic pollution is one of the most pressing environmental issues facing our oceans today."
Strong: "Every liter of water we pulled out of Nahant Bay last summer had plastic in it. Every single one."

**End when the point lands.** No summary paragraph. If the last body paragraph completes the argument, that's the ending. If you want a beat of closure, end on a kicker (delivery-methods.md, section 5): an image, a consequence, or the sharpest short sentence in the piece. New material, not recap.

## 9. Adverbs and adjectives are on probation (King, Strunk & White)

An adverb usually confesses that the verb was weak: "walked slowly" is trudged, "said angrily" is snapped. An unearned adjective is a claim without evidence: "innovative design" says nothing; "a design no other team ran" says something. Default: cut the modifier, strengthen the noun or verb. Keep the modifiers that carry real information ("aluminum frame," "second attempt").

## 10. Match the writer, not a house style

When the writing goes out under someone's name, it has to sound like a plausible them:

- Vocabulary ceiling: a high schooler's essay can be sharp without "epistemological." Sharp and simple beats impressive and stiff at every level.
- Sample their voice: reuse the user's own phrasings, sentence habits, and typical punctuation from the conversation.
- Match the medium: a Discord announcement, a college essay, and a cold email to a professor are three different registers. All three can follow every rule in this skill. The rules govern quality, not formality.

## The final checklist

Run before delivering anything:

1. Can I state the piece's point in one sentence, and does the piece actually make it?
2. Did I pick a delivery structure on purpose, and does the piece follow it?
3. Read aloud: does every sentence pass?
4. Slop audit: any 150-word stretch containing 2+ banned patterns? Any em dashes at all?
5. Is there at least one proper noun, number, or physical detail per paragraph?
6. Is there at least one sentence someone could disagree with?
7. Do sentence and paragraph lengths vary?
8. Does it end on new material, not a recap?
9. The editor pass: would a newspaper editor or an English teacher who's seen forty AI essays circle anything?
10. Did I cut 10% on the final pass?
