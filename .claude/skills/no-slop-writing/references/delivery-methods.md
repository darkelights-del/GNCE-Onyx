# Delivery Methods

Structure is the half of writing that sentence-level rules can't fix. A piece can have zero clichés and still fail because the information arrives in the wrong order for the job. This file is the toolkit: what each structure is, when it wins, and how to run it. Pick one deliberately in workflow step 2.

## 1. How to choose

Ask what the reader needs first and what they'll do with it.

- Reader is busy and needs to act (reply, approve, show up): **BLUF**.
- Reader wants the facts of something that happened: **inverted pyramid**.
- Reader is browsing and must be earned: **anecdotal lede + nut graf**.
- Reader must be persuaded to change something: **SCQA**.
- Reader is grading an argument about a text or data: **PEEL** paragraphs inside a thesis-driven essay.
- Reader is scanning accomplishments in six seconds: **XYZ**.
- Reader needs to understand how something unfolded or works: **narrative/chronological**.
- The topic itself is abstract (justice, resilience, convergence, entropy): any of the above, plus the **ladder of abstraction** running through it.

Structures can nest. A blog post might open with an anecdotal lede, carry an SCQA argument in the middle, and land on a kicker.

## 2. BLUF: Bottom Line Up Front

The military memo standard. First sentence states the conclusion or the ask; everything after is support the reader can skip.

Weak email: three paragraphs of context ending with "so I was wondering if it might be possible to move the deadline."
BLUF email: "Can we move the deadline to Friday the 12th? Two reasons." Then the reasons, shortest first.

Use for every email, Slack/Discord message, memo, and request. The politeness lives in tone, not in burying the ask. A professor cold-email still opens with who you are and what you want within the first two sentences.

## 3. Inverted pyramid

The newsroom structure: most important facts first, then supporting detail in descending order of importance, background last. The classic lede answers who, what, when, where, why in one sentence under about 30 words. The test of a good pyramid: an editor can cut from the bottom, paragraph by paragraph, and the story survives.

Use for announcements, updates, recaps, press releases, and any web writing where readers skim. Design the tail to be expendable: if deleting the last paragraph would leave the piece incomplete, that material belongs higher up.

## 4. Anecdotal lede + nut graf

The feature-writing structure. Open with a specific scene, moment, or person (the lede), then a short paragraph that answers "why am I reading this?" by connecting the scene to the larger point (the nut graf). Then the body delivers.

Lede: "The third time the pump clogged, we were a mile offshore and the sun was going down."
Nut graf: "Clogs turned out to be the whole ballgame. Every design decision we made after June traces back to that evening."

Use for blog posts, magazine-style pieces, college essays, and speeches. The discipline: the nut graf must arrive fast (within the first 10 to 15 percent of the piece) or the reader suspects the anecdote is stalling.

## 5. Hourglass and the kicker

The hourglass opens as an inverted pyramid (facts first), then narrows into a chronological narrative once the reader is oriented. Good for event recaps where both the outcome and the story matter: give the result up top, then tell how it happened.

The kicker is the last line of a piece, and it deserves as much work as the lede. A kicker is new material with snap: a final quote, a small ironic detail, a consequence, a callback to the opening scene. It is never a summary. If the natural last line summarizes, delete it and see if the line above it kicks.

## 6. SCQA: Situation, Complication, Question, Answer

The consulting/persuasion structure (from Barbara Minto's pyramid principle). Situation: the stable context everyone agrees on. Complication: what changed or broke. Question: the question the complication forces (often left implicit). Answer: your recommendation, followed by grouped supporting reasons.

Situation: "The cafeteria produces about 40 pounds of food waste a day, all landfilled."
Complication: "MassDEP's new limits put the school out of compliance by next fall."
Answer: "A three-bin composting program with Black Earth Compost fixes it for under $2,000 a year. Here's the plan."

Use for proposals, grant applications, pitches, and any writing whose job is a yes. The power is that the reader arrives at the question themselves one beat before you answer it.

## 7. PEEL: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link

The analytical-paragraph structure English teachers actually grade against. Point: the paragraph's claim, first sentence. Evidence: the quote, data, or scene that supports it. Explanation: the part students skip and AI fakes, where you show *how* the evidence proves the point. Link: connect forward to the thesis or the next idea.

The explanation is where the grade lives and where AI writing collapses into "this shows that the author emphasizes the importance of..." Real explanation names the mechanism: what the word choice does, what the number implies, what would be different if the evidence said otherwise. One well-explained piece of evidence beats three quoted-and-abandoned ones.

Use inside essays for English, history, and DBQs. Vary the surface so paragraphs don't march in identical PEEL lockstep; the logic stays, the phrasing varies.

## 8. They Say / I Say

The academic argument move (Graff and Birkenstein): state the existing view fairly and specifically, then position your claim against it. "Most coverage treats nearshore plastic as a proximity problem: trash concentrates near outfalls. Our sampling says the currents matter more than the sources."

This is the honest version of what slop fakes with "some critics argue." The difference is specificity: name who says it, or at least state the view sharply enough that its holders would recognize it. Use in research writing, debate cases, op-eds, and any essay with a thesis someone could dispute.

## 9. XYZ formula

Google's bullet structure for resumes and activity lists: "Accomplished X, as measured by Y, by doing Z." Result first, proof second, method third.

Task bullet: "Responsible for team fundraising."
XYZ bullet: "Raised $4,200 for robotics season (2x prior year) by pitching 11 local businesses in person."

Two rules from practice: every bullet needs the Y (a number, a before/after, a ranking, a rate) even when the metric is rough, and the surface wording should vary bullet to bullet so a list doesn't read as one template stamped eight times. Use for resumes, Common App activity descriptions, award applications, and LinkedIn.

## 10. Narrative and chronological

Tell it in order when the order is the point: postmortems, build logs, process explanations, personal essays about a change over time. Narrative needs the story elements (a person who wants something, an obstacle, a turn), not just a timeline. "First we did A, then B, then C" is a list wearing a trenchcoat; "we did A, it failed for a reason we didn't understand until B" is a story.

Compress ruthlessly. Skip the boring middle. Readers forgive jumps in time; they don't forgive being walked through every uneventful week.

## 11. Writing about abstract ideas

Abstract topics (resilience, fairness, convergence, scalability, grief) are where slop concentrates, because vagueness hides easily in them. The tools:

**The ladder of abstraction.** Every concept sits on a ladder from concrete (this pump, this Tuesday, this $90 bill) to abstract (efficiency, perseverance, systems thinking). Good writing climbs the ladder constantly and never stays at the top for more than a sentence or two. State the abstraction, then immediately drop to a rung the reader can see. If a paragraph has been abstract for three sentences, the next sentence must be a person, an object, a number, or a moment.

Stuck at the top: "Resilience means adapting to setbacks and maintaining commitment to long-term goals despite adversity."
Climbing down: "Resilience, for our team, was a rule: any mechanism that broke twice got redesigned, not repaired. The intake broke twice in October. We redesigned it over a weekend and it never broke again."

**Define by example and by contrast.** The fastest way to pin an abstraction is one clear positive example plus one near-miss. "Convergence zones are where surface currents collide and fold under, trapping floaters. A river mouth is not one; the water there spreads out instead of folding."

**Analogy, used once and dropped.** One sharp analogy makes an abstract mechanism visible ("Langmuir circulation works like the seam where two conveyor belts meet"). Extending the analogy for a full paragraph turns it into a crutch. Land it, then return to the real thing.

**The consequences test.** If an abstract claim is real, something observable follows from it. Force every abstraction to name its consequence: "the design is scalable" becomes "the design is scalable: adding a second pump needs one bracket and no firmware changes." A claim with no nameable consequence gets cut.

**Process abstract ideas before writing them.** When the user hands you a fuzzy concept to write about, do the ladder work first: ask (or infer from context) what concrete instance made them care about this idea, and build the piece around that instance. The abstraction becomes the frame; the instance carries the weight.
